About Me

My name is Alex.

I came to coding through poetry – specifically, through trying to make a transcription of some pages from one poet’s densely overwritten notebooks. I turned to LaTeX to do this and was fascinated at how, on the one hand, the work I was trying to do was very much not what the software had been designed for. On the other hand, it was evidently a powerful and flexible tool and there was a brilliant, inventive community ready to adapt it to various ends.

For a long time I believed that by studying and working in the arts and humanities I had barred myself from options that fall more under STEM. This feeling deterred me from pursuing coding for a long time, but my experience with LaTeX began to make me feel that my different training might not be a hindrance; instead, it might mean that I have different and valuable skills to contribute.

I’ve worked as a researcher and teacher at university level. My research has focussed on the avant-garde in London’s Jewish East End in the early twentieth century. While my work took place within UCL’s English department, it was clear to me that I also needed to learn Yiddish to do the topic justice. I was able to gain enough proficiency in the language to read relevant and exciting texts that had not yet been translated into English. I was able to answer research problems that would have otherwise been insoluble. In addition, I also took great joy in experimenting with writing in the language and translating in and out of it.

The process of learning a language and playing around with its possibilities is a great joy to me, and it is a joy that I have also found as I’ve started to learn to code. That’s why I chose to have a switch to change between English and Yiddish on this application website: as a tribute to a language that has brought me great pleasure and which has made me feel more at home learning HTML, CSS and JS. While the Yiddish on this website is mostly my translation of the English, the equivalent Yiddish for this and the Why FAC? sections are excerpts from Nokhem Stif’s essay on the language.

Through attending workshops and meet-ups organised by FAC, I have started to gain confidence while recognising how far I still have to go – a prospect as exciting as it is daunting!

Why FAC?

FAC combines several features that are important to me. It takes seriously its role as a social actor. I am keen to do work that is ethically and politically meaningful as well as intellectually stimulating. I have been especially impressed by the work of Forensic Architecture and Our World in Data using digital tools. Os Keyes’ writing has also been influential in making me question the limits as well as the advantages of such approaches. I believe that FAC’s course will provide a context that will help me to pursue this aim, partly through creating a community of individuals who hold similar priorities. I hope that it will also help me to continue to build a sophisticated picture of what can be achieved and where best to focus one’s energies.

There are other elements of FAC that appeal strongly to me. FAC presents itself in a very different way from other coding bootcamps. It does not promise massive transformations within an improbably short period of time. It emphasises collaboration as key to the practice of teaching and learning. It is open with its resources and aims. It is attentive to flexibility and to employment prospects: the balance of pre-apprenticeship and apprenticeship sections seems especially thoughtful. I admire it as a project and would be both honoured and delighted to be selected as a member of its next cohort.
